The key design shift
Your first plan treats the site as a set of pages.
The better plan is to treat it as a framing device for a practice.
That means the site needs two things at once:
	1	Conventional portfolio performance so a hiring panel, programmer, collaborator, journalist, or funder can instantly find the right proof.
	2	A distinctive mental atmosphere so the visitor feels, within seconds, that Chris is not a generic academic or a generic creative.
That atmosphere is where Adams / Coupland / Rushkoff help.
	•	From Adams, borrow playful intelligence and systems-awareness.
	•	From Coupland, borrow typographic confidence, cultural sharpness, and language-as-design.
	•	From Rushkoff, borrow humane digital clarity and anti-noise restraint.
Those are not “themes.” They are operating principles.
What was making the earlier direction feel generic
The old architecture becomes bland when it defaults to these moves:
	•	standard hero + headshot + credentials
	•	standard academic nav labels
	•	standard project cards
	•	standard “research / teaching / writing” separation
	•	standard institutional tone
Those things are useful, but if they are the dominant experience, Chris starts to look like everyone else with a university page and a Squarespace portfolio.
That is especially risky because his current web presence is fragmented already: his site underperforms as a central hub while his institutional and publishing presence carry much of the credibility.
So don’t throw away the portfolio structure. Instead, embed it inside a more specific editorial system.
The right model
I’d redesign the site around this idea:
Christopher Hogg is a writer-researcher building stories, teaching frameworks, and public arguments about how technology reshapes human creativity.
That one sentence can hold:
	•	plays
	•	podcasts
	•	memoir
	•	AI and education writing
	•	talks
	•	teaching
	•	research
It also matches what the public record already shows: creative practice, AI-in-education/public writing, memoir on Substack, and university-based research/teaching.
The site structure I’d keep
Your structure is basically right. I would keep the main categories, but sharpen the naming and hierarchy.
Instead of:
Home / Research / Projects / Teaching / Writing / Media / About / Contact
I’d go with:
Home / Work / Writing / Teaching / Speaking / About / Contact
And let Research live inside About and Teaching, unless he needs a dedicated research page for academic audiences.
Why? Because “Research” as a top-level nav item often pushes a site into generic academic territory. Chris’s work is stronger when framed through outputs and ideas, not just institutional categorisation.
Then inside Work, split into:
	•	Creative Work
	•	Selected Projects
	•	Research-led Experiments
That lets Rathband, Cassie & Corey, 9 Holidays, and future AI/creative-tech work sit together without forcing a hard false split.
How to make it distinct without hurting usability
This is the important bit.
The distinctiveness should come from five design layers, not from eccentric layout.
1. Voice
This is the biggest missed opportunity in most portfolio sites.
Chris’s site copy should not sound like university boilerplate. It should sound like someone who:
	•	understands performance
	•	has lived experience
	•	is alert to absurdity
	•	thinks critically about technology
	•	can move between seriousness and wit
That’s where Adams and Coupland are useful: not as references, but as permission to let the site’s microcopy and framing feel authored.
For example, instead of bland summaries like:
“This project explores…”
use lines with angle and compression, like:
“A podcast musical built for the smartphone, made to speak to teenagers about illness, intimacy, and the difficulty of being seen.”
Or:
“A memoir, an archive, and a test of what happens when trauma meets synthetic memory.”
That gives portfolio clarity, but it feels written by a writer.
2. Typography
If you want distinctive without chaos, typography is where to spend the energy.
I’d use:
	•	a serious, literary serif for long reading and project intros
	•	a clean, modern sans for nav, metadata, project stats, labels, captions
This lets the site switch naturally between:
	•	playwright
	•	memoirist
	•	lecturer
	•	creative technologist
That’s far more effective than visual gimmicks.
3. Metadata as design
One of the best ways to make a portfolio feel intelligent is to turn information into part of the visual system.
Every project should have a structured “signal box” near the top:
	•	Format
	•	Year
	•	Collaborators
	•	Funding / Commission
	•	Awards
	•	Audience / Reach
	•	Research question
	•	Listen / Read / Watch
This solves the portfolio problem elegantly. It gives panels and commissioners immediate proof, while also making the page feel more curated and rigorous.
For Chris, that matters because works like Rathband and 9 Holidays are not just creative outputs; they are also research-bearing artifacts. His Royal Holloway profile and publication record already support that framing.
4. Editorial pacing
Don’t make every page a stack of cards.
Use rhythm:
	•	large title
	•	short premise
	•	metadata block
	•	one strong visual or audio embed
	•	a compact essay paragraph
	•	selected outcomes
	•	related writing / talks / context
That makes the site feel more like a curated notebook of major works than a directory.
5. Controlled strangeness
This is where the influence comes in.
You want moments that feel slightly unexpected:
	•	section labels like Current Signal, Selected Works, Notes from the Edge of Teaching, Fragments
	•	small dry lines of microcopy
	•	recurring tags like PLAY / PODCAST / MEMOIR / AI / CLASSROOM / TRAUMA / COMEDY / SYSTEMS
	•	occasional pull-quotes that work like thought interruptions
These touches make the site feel authored. But the page architecture remains clean.
What the homepage should do
The homepage should not just say “here is Christopher Hogg.”
It should answer, fast:
	•	Who is he?
	•	What kind of work does he make?
	•	Why does it matter now?
	•	What proof is there?
	•	Where do I go next?
So I’d structure the homepage like this:
Hero
Christopher Hogg Writer, educator, and creative technologist exploring how stories, classrooms, and machines shape one another.
Then one short paragraph with institutional grounding: Royal Holloway / Media Arts / current themes in AI, creativity, storytelling.
That institutional grounding matters because it immediately upgrades credibility for academic and commissioning audiences. His current university profile and recent Royal Holloway feature make that association important and current.
Then three blocks
	•	Featured Work
	•	Latest Writing / Talks
	•	Teaching & Research
Not equal-sized cards — more like an editorial layout with one dominant item and two supporting tracks.
Then a “selected proof” strip
For example:
	•	BBC Podcast of the Year
	•	Times Higher Education
	•	Royal Holloway
	•	Arts Council England
	•	British Podcast Awards
	•	StoryFutures / CoSTAR if appropriate
This is a portfolio move, not a branding move. It says: this practice has recognition and context.
Then a living feed
A few recent items:
	•	latest article
	•	latest talk
	•	latest Substack post
	•	current project
That helps the site feel alive and current.

The design style in one sentence
Editorial, exact, humane, and slightly disobedient.
That’s the non-generic version of “clean and modern.”
Concrete visual system
If I were art directing this, I’d choose:
	•	Background: warm white or pale grey, not stark white
	•	Text: charcoal, not pure black
	•	Accent: one intelligent synthetic colour — cobalt, signal red, or acid green used sparingly
	•	Typography: one serif, one sans
	•	Imagery: sparse, chosen, documentary not decorative
	•	Motion: almost none, except soft reveals and state changes
	•	Shapes: mostly rectangular, grid-led, no blob-branding
	•	Texture: occasional rule lines, metadata dividers, archival captions
That gives you:
	•	portfolio seriousness
	•	literary sharpness
	•	digital present-tense
	•	no trend-chasing
The real lesson from Adams / Coupland / Rushkoff
Here’s the usable translation:
	•	Adams says: a site can be intelligent and funny without becoming cute.
	•	Coupland says: text can be image, category, texture, and mood.
	•	Rushkoff says: digital design should help a person think, not pressure them to perform.
Applied to Chris, that becomes:
	•	make the site witty, not quirky
	•	make it typographic, not decorative
	•	make it clear, not corporate
	•	make it human, not “personal brand”
	•	make it portfolio-useful, not portfolio-generic
