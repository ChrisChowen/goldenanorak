Podcast of the Year

In July 2010, PC David Rathband was shot and blinded by Raoul Moat. What followed was not just a crime story but a collision between trauma, celebrity, social media, and the impossibility of private grief in a public age. Rathband explores that collision through three episodes designed specifically for visually impaired audiences—a form constraint that became the project's defining aesthetic.

The BBC described the production as "technically stunning." The podcast reached #1 on iTunes and received over 30,000 downloads. Douglas Rushkoff—media theorist, author, documentary filmmaker—appears as himself throughout the series.

Can digital forces be "tragic" in the classical dramatic sense? That was the question underneath everything.

Research Questions

  • How does social media mediate trauma, disability, and masculinity?
  • What happens when public and private identity collapse in the digital space?
  • Can digital forces be "tragic" in the classical dramatic sense?
  • How do new narrative forms emerge from new technologies?

Context

Rathband began as a stage play before being adapted into podcast form. Christopher also spoke at TEDxUAL about the work and the role social media played in the events it depicts. The project sits at the intersection of creative practice and practice-based research—a piece of work that is simultaneously art, argument, and evidence.

TEDxUAL: Social Media and Digital Tragedy

At TEDxUAL, Christopher presented the research underpinning Rathband—examining how social media platforms transform private trauma into public spectacle, and whether the mechanics of virality, algorithmic amplification, and digital mob behaviour can produce consequences that are genuinely tragic in the classical dramatic sense. The talk draws on the Rathband case as a lens through which to understand the collision between old human vulnerabilities and new technological forces.
